Technofeudalism: 1

Technofeudalism is a system in which large tech companies hold a vast majority of power and retain control and influence over their users and customers. This term originates from historical feudalism, a system where kings, at the top of the feudal hierarchy, owned all the land and granted portions of it to members of society in exchange for military loyalty or service. The nobles who received land would, in turn, distribute portions to lower-ranking participants like serfs, in exchange for services like protection.

This power was decentralized in the sense that once the king distributed land, he no longer controlled it directly. The recipients of land could continue this process, ensuring that every class of society had a role, and the system worked smoothly through subjective value exchanges. The downfall of feudalism was driven by the introduction of coinage—physical currency became a means of wealth accumulation, replacing land. This allowed for private wealth accumulation, creating a system where individuals were no longer dependent on land controlled by higher classes, leading to more transactional freedom.

Today, we see an oligopoly of major tech companies like Google, Meta, and TikTok. These companies consolidate their power by making us dependent on their platforms and payment gateways for communication, social validation, and commerce. They turn our usage into data, which they sell to third parties for profit, leaving us with little control over how that data is handled. Additionally, users on these platforms don't own anything, including the means of production. The only ownership claim users have is their username, which isn't truly theirs—it can be taken away by the platform if deemed necessary. In a way, this mirrors the relationship between kings and land ownership, with platforms controlling usernames in a similar way. Ultimately, users are not owners or producers—they are merely participants. While users can generate attention and monetize it, they remain bound to the role of "user."

Finally, we are all subject to the algorithm. Your content won't be seen unless it adheres to the platform's criteria, and the algorithm can change at any moment, potentially disrupting your content's reach. Interestingly enough, cryptocurrency has the potential to become the same catalyst that coinage once was. Cryptocurrency operates as an open, decentralized ledger, allowing participation from anyone with basic internet access, regardless of background or currency. Its peer-to-peer nature removes intermediaries, making transactions and tasks frictionless. The adoption of cryptocurrency will bring challenges, and I'm sure cybercrime will rise at a linear rate to the rate of adoption of cryptocurrency. Although it will also create new sectors and job opportunities as individuals develop ways to protect and manage this open ledger—much like how nobles once hired serfs to protect their land, the cycle begins anew.

Neural pathways, thought patterns, and compartmentalization: 7

Neural pathway: is the connection formed by axons that project from neurons to make synapses onto neurons in another location, to enable neurotransmission (the sending of a signal from one region of the nervous system to another). In this text, I'm gonna write about how to use your neural pathways (pertaining to your thoughts and thoughts alone) and how understanding the anatomical nature of neural pathways will aid you in life.

With neural pathways, you can have a very strong pathway, or a weak one. The more you exercise a pathway, the stronger it gets, let me break it down like so. Imagine you are sledding, and you have just created a new path. The first few times you go down that new path, you'll be slow and might not make it all the way down. Though, the more you go down that path, the easier it gets, and it gets better defined and molded by your sled, and in turn, you'll be quicker and quicker every time you go down it.

This is the same for your neural pathways (thoughts). If you think a thought 1,000 times, you arrive at the same conclusion or emotional state almost immediately due to the repetition of thought. Consequently, every time you think of a thought, the easier it gets to think it, and the path to that thought gets less resistant every time. This is where you have to be careful. If you're thinking positive thoughts all the time, then it's no problem, of course. Though, if you're thinking negative thoughts, then doing this will result in you getting stuck in negative loop(s) and hence creating a negative life or experience for yourself.

Compartmentalization: noun 1. the division of something into sections or categories.

Compartmentalization is more advanced than what I've just written about, but it's close enough to the topic to mention it if you want to learn it outside of this text. Mental compartmentalization is simply sectioning off thoughts for different occasions or experiences. This helps you change your state to mold to every scenario you find yourself in. Napoleon allegedly was really good at this. Here's a quote from him:

"Different subjects and different affairs are arranged in my head as in a cupboard. When I wish to interrupt one train of thought, I shut that drawer and open another. Do I wish to sleep? I simply close all the drawers, and there I am — asleep." Napoleon: A Life, Andrew Roberts

The Slingshot: 17

The slingshot is a strategy to access an alternative and superior path for you to embark on and experience. This strategy shares ingredients and core elements of monasticism and asceticism, depositing productivity in the direction of your goals and mitigating any and all neutralizing or entropic actions or effects. This translates to all focus work/dedicated hours and energy toward this goal or ambition with singleness of purpose.

To paint out the strategy in action: when you focus your attention exclusively on actions that move you in the direction of the attainment of your goal, you are, in essence, pulling your slingshot back. What does it mean to "let go" of your slingshot? Letting go means indulging in those things that you know do not contribute to the achievement of your goal—i.e., having a glass of wine, skipping a focus work session, haphazardly doing a task, having a cheat meal, partying, taking unnecessary breaks, etc.—or indulging in the rewards of your hard work or the fruits of your labor.

When you let go of the slingshot, you will be propelled to the exact destination in proportion to the force you've used to pull the slingshot back. The more you pull your slingshot back, the further you'll go—and when you let go, you reset the hard work and dedicated energy you've accumulated over that period of time. The further your slingshot is pulled back, the more tension and strength is required to keep pulling it back. Do not give in to the release of the slingshot for any reason other than the fact that you have already attained the goal.

Distraction: 19

We live in a world where distractions are virtually everywhere. Adopt this lens, go somewhere (physically, or pull out your phone), and observe—you'll find that everything is pulling your attention away from you. Recognize that many people always have something playing: when they go for a walk, they have music or a podcast on; in the gym, they listen to music; before going to bed, they watch videos; and when they wake up, they go on their phone. This constantly mutes your own thoughts and plugs in external indoctrination or noise, whether it's directed consciously or not.

We have lost much of our introspection and self-awareness due to this new paradigm that feeds economically and monetarily on your attention, which you accept. The cycle begins as follows: content is produced and algorithmically designed for consumption by an audience. This audience then willfully consumes that content and unknowingly energizes it by giving it what it needs in the form of viewership and engagement.

The reason why this is a problem is that it takes people's personal time, attention, and energy away from themselves. While an equilibrium of media consumption could be reached, nowadays it is not. You could name a million ways people can better themselves or at least avoid entropy by limiting distractions: they can spend this time thinking about their own goals and aspirations, plan out what they want to do with their life in the near or distant future (and how to do so), or reflect on their own character and daily experiences to improve for the next day.

Human beings are hardwired to release endorphins after rigorous work, and once you put in that effort, you'll feel those endorphins for an extended period of time—far longer than the manufactured endorphins you get from media. Why do you think you accidentally open apps on your phone and start scrolling, or find yourself scrolling without realizing? Your brain is searching for some dopamine. Self-censor, and do not accept fake rewards.
